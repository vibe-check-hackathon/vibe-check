# Founder World: adaptive jargon, knowledge and learning assessment

**Version:** 1.0  
**Information checked:** 21 July 2026  
**Audience:** Aspiring founders, first-time founders, experienced operators, interview designers, accelerators and investors  
**Purpose:** Give founders a clear map of the startup world, diagnose what they already know, teach only what is useful, and observe learning behavior without confusing prior exposure with founder potential.

> **Core rule:** Startup vocabulary is learnable domain knowledge. It is not intelligence, personality, curiosity or founder quality. Prior knowledge should personalize the learning path—not determine whether someone deserves an opportunity.

## 1. What the system should produce

The founder should leave with:

1. a map of the founder world;
2. a domain-by-domain knowledge profile;
3. explanations of unfamiliar terms in plain language;
4. scenario practice matched to venture stage and sector;
5. a learning plan containing no more than three immediate priorities;
6. an optional record of learning progress;
7. no stigmatizing global label such as “weak founder” or “low curiosity.”

The evaluator may receive, with the founder's knowledge and appropriate consent:

- demonstrated knowledge by domain;
- confidence calibration;
- ability to apply concepts to a new case;
- learning gain after instruction;
- evidence of updating after a misconception;
- uncertainty and test coverage.

The evaluator must not receive sensitive learning telemetry, speculative personality inferences or a hidden “founder IQ.”

## 2. The founder-world map

| Domain | Central founder question | Foundation vocabulary | Applied capability |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Problem and customer** | Whose important problem are we solving? | customer segment, ICP, persona, pain point, job-to-be-done, early adopter | Conduct non-leading discovery and distinguish evidence from enthusiasm |
| **2. Market and competition** | Is the opportunity large, reachable and changing? | TAM, SAM, SOM, beachhead, category, substitute, incumbent, moat | Build a bottom-up market estimate and identify real alternatives |
| **3. Product and validation** | What is the smallest credible test? | hypothesis, assumption, MVP, prototype, proof of concept, pilot, product–market fit | Choose a test that can disconfirm a critical assumption |
| **4. Business model** | How does the venture create, deliver and capture value? | revenue model, pricing, margin, marketplace, subscription, usage-based, licensing | Connect customer value, price, cost structure and repeatability |
| **5. Go-to-market and sales** | How will customers discover, evaluate, buy and adopt? | GTM, channel, funnel, pipeline, lead, conversion, sales cycle, champion, economic buyer | Design a stage-appropriate acquisition and sales process |
| **6. Metrics and unit economics** | Does growth create or destroy value? | CAC, LTV, gross margin, churn, retention, cohort, ARPU/ARR/MRR, payback | Calculate metrics, state assumptions and recognize misleading aggregates |
| **7. Finance and operations** | How long can the company operate and what must happen before cash runs out? | burn, runway, cash flow, forecast, budget, accounts receivable/payable, break-even | Build a cash-based operating plan with milestones and downside cases |
| **8. Funding and ownership** | Which capital fits the venture and what does it cost? | bootstrapping, grant, angel, VC, debt, SAFE, convertible note/loan, priced round, valuation, dilution, cap table, liquidation preference, pro rata | Compare instruments and model ownership/economic consequences |
| **9. Team and governance** | Who decides, owns and remains accountable? | founder agreement, vesting, cliff, option pool, board, fiduciary duty, reserved matter, deadlock | Create clear roles, decision rights and conflict processes |
| **10. Legal, IP and compliance** | What rights and obligations could block the venture? | entity, incorporation, IP, assignment, license, patent, trademark, trade secret, FTO, privacy, securities, employment status | Identify when specialist advice is required and preserve evidence/rights |
| **11. Technology and risk** | Can the solution work safely, reliably and at scale? | architecture, technical debt, security, reliability, regulatory pathway, TRL, validation, quality system | Link technical risks to commercial milestones and failure tests |
| **12. Ecosystem and scaling** | Which external resources create the next measurable result? | incubator, accelerator, angel network, mentor, advisor, technology transfer, channel partner, strategic investor, term sheet, due diligence | Select and convert a relevant opportunity without chasing prestige |

This map combines practical business fundamentals with the European Commission's [EntreComp framework](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/entrecomp-entrepreneurship-competence-framework_en), which organizes entrepreneurship as a lifelong competence rather than a single personality type. EntreComp is a reference framework—not a validated founder-selection test.

## 3. Essential glossary

### Problem, customer and market

| Term | Plain-language meaning | Common mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer segment** | A group with meaningfully similar needs or buying behavior | Defining it only by broad demographics |
| **ICP** | Ideal Customer Profile: the type of customer most likely to receive and create value | Treating every possible user as ideal |
| **User / buyer / economic buyer** | User operates the product; buyer runs the purchase; economic buyer controls the budget | Assuming they are always the same person |
| **Pain point** | A costly, frequent or consequential customer problem | Calling a minor preference a pain point |
| **Job-to-be-done** | The progress a customer is trying to make in a situation | Describing product features instead of the customer's job |
| **Early adopter** | A customer willing to accept early limitations because the problem is urgent | Anyone who says an idea sounds interesting |
| **TAM** | Total theoretical market demand under stated assumptions | Presenting a huge top-down industry number as reachable revenue |
| **SAM** | Portion of TAM served by the current product and business model | Ignoring geography, regulation or channel constraints |
| **SOM** | Realistically obtainable portion of SAM in a defined period | Selecting an arbitrary percentage |
| **Beachhead market** | Narrow initial market chosen to establish strong adoption and references | Launching broadly to avoid making a choice |
| **Substitute** | Any alternative way the customer solves or tolerates the problem | Counting only products that look technically similar |
| **Moat** | A defensible advantage that becomes difficult to copy or displace | Treating a feature or first-mover claim as automatically durable |

### Product and evidence

| Term | Plain-language meaning | Common mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Assumption** | Something that must be true for the venture to work | Leaving it implicit and therefore untestable |
| **Hypothesis** | A falsifiable prediction connecting an action or condition to an observable result | Writing a hope with no failure threshold |
| **Prototype** | Representation used to learn about design, function or interaction | Building production infrastructure before learning |
| **MVP** | Minimum viable product or test that generates decision-relevant learning | “Bad first version” with no explicit learning goal |
| **Proof of principle** | Evidence that the underlying scientific/technical mechanism can work | Claiming product readiness |
| **Proof of concept** | Evidence that a defined implementation can work in a relevant context | Claiming scalable production or market demand |
| **Pilot** | Limited real-world implementation with defined parties, scope and outcomes | Free, indefinite usage with no success criteria |
| **Product–market fit** | Strong, repeatable evidence that a defined market values and retains the product | One famous customer, press attention or revenue bought through unsustainable effort |
| **Vanity metric** | A number that looks positive but does not reliably guide a decision | Reporting registrations without activation or retention |
| **Cohort** | A group sharing a start period or meaningful characteristic, tracked over time | Mixing old and new customers into one average |

### Business model, sales and growth

| Term | Plain-language meaning | Common mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue model** | How the company earns money | Confusing it with the full business model |
| **Business model** | How the venture creates, delivers and captures value | Describing only pricing |
| **GTM** | Go-to-market: how a specific market is reached, sold, onboarded and retained | “We will use social media” |
| **Channel** | Route through which a customer is reached or served | Assuming a partner will sell without incentives and enablement |
| **Funnel** | Stages through which prospects become and remain customers | Optimizing top-of-funnel volume while ignoring retention |
| **Pipeline** | Qualified potential deals and their stages, value and probability | Counting every contact as a qualified opportunity |
| **Champion** | Person inside an organization who actively helps the purchase succeed | Confusing friendliness with influence |
| **Sales cycle** | Time from qualified opportunity to purchase/close | Ignoring procurement, security, legal and budget timing |
| **Activation** | First behavior showing the user has reached meaningful value | Using account creation as activation by default |
| **Retention** | Continued use or purchasing by the same cohort | Measuring only new acquisition |
| **Churn** | Customers or recurring revenue lost during a period | Mixing logo churn and revenue churn |

### Metrics, finance and ownership

| Term | Plain-language meaning | Common mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Gross margin** | Revenue minus direct cost of delivering it, divided by revenue | Excluding material direct delivery costs to look more attractive |
| **CAC** | Customer-acquisition cost under a stated method and period | Dividing only ad spend by new customers while excluding sales cost |
| **LTV** | Expected gross-profit contribution over the customer relationship | Using revenue, infinite retention or optimistic cohorts |
| **CAC payback** | Time required for customer gross profit to recover acquisition cost | Using bookings instead of realized contribution |
| **MRR / ARR** | Monthly/annual recurring revenue normalized from recurring contracts | Including one-off services or non-recurring commitments |
| **Burn** | Net cash consumed over a period | Using accounting loss without reconciling cash |
| **Runway** | Time until cash is exhausted under explicit burn assumptions | Treating current burn as fixed despite planned hires or revenue risk |
| **Break-even** | Point where the defined revenue/contribution covers the defined costs | Failing to say whether this means cash, operating or accounting break-even |
| **Cap table** | Record/model of ownership and rights across securities | Showing percentages without options, convertibles or scenario assumptions |
| **Pre-money valuation** | Agreed company value immediately before new investment | Adding the new cash twice |
| **Post-money valuation** | Pre-money valuation plus new primary investment, subject to deal mechanics | Ignoring option-pool or convertible treatment |
| **Dilution** | Reduction in an existing holder's percentage when new securities are issued | Assuming lower percentage always means lower economic value |
| **SAFE** | Contract giving future equity rights under specified conversion terms; not ordinary equity at signing | Treating every SAFE as identical or as debt |
| **Convertible note/loan** | Debt intended or permitted to convert into equity under stated triggers | Ignoring maturity, interest, cap, discount and priority |
| **Priced round** | Equity financing in which share price and company valuation are set | Assuming the headline valuation describes every economic term |
| **Liquidation preference** | Contractual rule allocating proceeds before or alongside common shareholders in specified exits | Looking only at ownership percentages |
| **Pro rata right** | Right to invest in a later round to preserve an ownership percentage | Calling it guaranteed allocation regardless of documents |
| **Term sheet** | Usually non-binding summary of principal transaction terms, with specified binding exceptions | Treating it as closed financing |
| **Due diligence** | Investigation of company, team, market, technology, legal and financial claims | Treating it as a formality after verbal interest |

### Team, legal and ecosystem

| Term | Plain-language meaning | Common mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Vesting** | Earning equity over time or milestones | Issuing permanent founder ownership without departure provisions |
| **Cliff** | Initial period before the first portion vests | Assuming it removes all negotiation or legal complexity |
| **Option pool** | Shares reserved for employee/advisor equity awards | Ignoring whether it is created before or after an investment |
| **Board** | Formal governing body with legal duties and defined powers | Treating it as an informal mentor group |
| **Advisor** | External person providing defined expertise or access | Granting equity for reputation without deliverables or vesting |
| **IP assignment** | Transfer of specified intellectual-property rights to another owner | Assuming payment for work automatically transfers every right |
| **License** | Permission to use defined rights under conditions without necessarily changing ownership | Saying “we own it” when the company has only limited rights |
| **Freedom to operate (FTO)** | Legal analysis of potential infringement risk for commercial activity | Equating a patent search with an FTO opinion |
| **Accelerator** | Usually fixed-term, selective venture-development program; economics vary | Assuming all accelerators invest or take equity |
| **Incubator** | Longer or flexible venture support, often tied to region or institution | Assuming admission is investment validation |
| **Angel** | Individual investing personal capital, often at early stage | Treating a mentor or introduction as an investor commitment |
| **VC** | Fund manager investing pooled capital under a strategy and return model | Assuming every VC fits every stage, sector or geography |
| **Strategic investor** | Corporate investor seeking financial and/or strategic benefit | Ignoring exclusivity, information and commercial conflicts |
| **Technology transfer office** | Institution function managing commercialization, IP and agreements | Assuming the founder controls institution-owned IP |

For practical reference, the [U.S. SBA Business Guide](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide) covers planning, formation, funding, operations and growth; Germany's [Gründerplattform](https://gruenderplattform.de/) supplies planning and finance tools; and the [NVCA model documents](https://nvca.org/model-legal-documents/) and Germany's [GESSI materials](https://www.business-angels.de/standardvertraege-gessi/) expose founders to the language used in venture transactions. These resources do not replace legal or financial advice.

## 4. Terms that differ between Germany and the United States

| Topic | Germany/common European usage | United States/common usage | Interview safeguard |
|---|---|---|---|
| Limited-liability entity | GmbH, UG (haftungsbeschränkt) | C corporation, LLC | Ask which jurisdiction and entity; do not grade one term as universally correct |
| Convertible financing | Wandeldarlehen / convertible loan | Convertible note; SAFE is also common | Ask about the actual contract mechanics |
| Equity financing documents | Beteiligungsvertrag, Gesellschaftervereinbarung, investment/shareholders' agreement | Stock Purchase Agreement, Investors' Rights Agreement, Voting Agreement, ROFR/Co-Sale | Test comprehension of consequences, not document-title recall |
| Shares/equity incentives | Geschäftsanteile, VSOP/ESOP and other plans | Common/preferred stock, stock options and other equity awards | Avoid assuming economic/legal equivalence |
| Public founder support | EXIST, Gründerplattform, regional/state programs | SBA/SBDC/SCORE, SBIR/STTR and agency/state programs | Match eligibility before testing program knowledge |
| Research commercialization | Hochschul-/Instituts-Transfer, EXIST/Fraunhofer pathways | University TTO, I-Corps, SBIR/STTR, federal-lab pathways | Separate IP ownership, program status and founder behavior |

## 5. Adaptive diagnostic architecture

### Phase A — founder-controlled setup

Ask only what is needed to personalize the test:

- founder stage: exploring, validating, operating, scaling or exited;
- venture type: software, marketplace, hardware, DeepTech, life science, consumer, services or other;
- primary country/jurisdiction;
- previous roles: founder, operator, researcher, investor, student or other;
- domains the founder wants to learn;
- preferred language, reading level, accessibility mode and time budget;
- whether the result is private learning only or will be shared.

Self-report controls routing but does not add or subtract points.

### Phase B — 12-item routing screen

Present one medium-difficulty item per domain. Ask the founder to give both an answer and confidence:

- **50%:** unsure/guessing;
- **70%:** more likely right than wrong;
- **90%:** confident;
- **99%:** nearly certain.

Routing rules:

| Response | Next action |
|---|---|
| Correct, 90–99% confidence | Move to application or advanced misconception item |
| Correct, 50–70% confidence | Confirm with a second item; then explain briefly |
| Incorrect, 50–70% confidence | Give a short explanation and foundation retrieval item |
| Incorrect, 90–99% confidence | Flag a confidently held misconception; explain and retest with a parallel scenario |
| “Not relevant to my venture” | Verify relevance; skip or replace without penalty |

### Phase C — adaptive learning loop

For each relevant domain:

1. **Retrieve:** ask a question before showing an explanation.
2. **Explain:** give a concise, jurisdiction-appropriate explanation.
3. **Apply:** use a scenario rather than a synonym question.
4. **Contrast:** show a tempting but wrong interpretation.
5. **Transfer:** ask the founder to apply it to their own venture or a novel case.
6. **Schedule:** revisit with a parallel item later rather than immediately repeating the same wording.

### Phase D — stopping rules

Stop a domain when one of these occurs:

- two correct application/transfer items at the target difficulty;
- two foundation errors followed by instruction and one successful retrieval, then schedule later review;
- the founder opts out or says the domain is irrelevant;
- the time budget is reached;
- confidence is too low to make a stable estimate.

The default session should take **12–20 minutes**, not an hour. A founder may voluntarily enter a deeper challenge mode.

## 6. Routing test: 12 questions

These questions form a transparent MVP. They require expert review and empirical calibration before consequential use.

### Questions

1. **Customer:** A founder asks ten prospective users, “Would you use an AI tool that saves you time?” Eight say yes. What is the strongest conclusion?  
   A. Product–market fit is established  
   B. Eight customers will pay  
   C. The result is weak because the question is hypothetical and leading  
   D. The TAM is large

2. **Market:** Which is the best basis for an initial SOM estimate?  
   A. One percent of a global industry report  
   B. Reachable accounts × realistic contract value × realistic conversion/capacity over a defined period  
   C. The largest competitor's revenue  
   D. The founder's five-year ambition

3. **Product:** Which experiment most directly tests whether clinics will change workflow for a new diagnostic tool?  
   A. A logo redesign  
   B. A general online interest survey  
   C. A defined pilot measuring adoption, workflow burden and decision impact  
   D. Filing a patent

4. **Business model:** A marketplace keeps 12% of each transaction. The 12% is best described as:  
   A. Gross margin  
   B. Take rate  
   C. Churn  
   D. Burn

5. **GTM:** In an enterprise sale, a product user likes the tool but cannot approve budget or security. What is still missing?  
   A. Only more product usage  
   B. Identification of the buying process, champion/economic buyer and approval stakeholders  
   C. A larger TAM slide  
   D. A lower valuation

6. **Metrics:** A company spends €60,000 on attributable sales and marketing in a quarter and gains 30 new customers under its chosen CAC method. What is CAC?  
   A. €500  
   B. €1,000  
   C. €2,000  
   D. Cannot ever be calculated

7. **Finance:** A company has €600,000 cash and currently consumes €75,000 net cash per month. Ignoring planned changes, approximate runway is:  
   A. 6 months  
   B. 8 months  
   C. 10 months  
   D. 12 months

8. **Funding:** A company worth €4 million pre-money receives €1 million in new primary equity, ignoring option-pool and other adjustments. What percentage does the new investor own immediately after closing?  
   A. 10%  
   B. 20%  
   C. 25%  
   D. 40%

9. **Team:** What is the main purpose of founder vesting?  
   A. Guarantee company success  
   B. Align ownership with continued contribution and govern early departure  
   C. Avoid a founder agreement  
   D. Eliminate dilution

10. **Legal/IP:** A university researcher is named as inventor on a patent application. What can safely be concluded?  
    A. The researcher personally owns all patent rights  
    B. The startup has freedom to operate  
    C. The person is named as inventor; ownership and commercial rights require separate verification  
    D. The patent is granted and enforceable

11. **Technology/risk:** A laboratory prototype works once under controlled conditions. What is the best next claim?  
    A. The technology is production-ready  
    B. The result is evidence, but reproducibility, relevant conditions, safety and scale remain to be tested  
    C. Product–market fit is established  
    D. Regulatory approval is likely

12. **Ecosystem:** A founder attended a Techstars Startup Weekend. Which description is accurate?  
    A. Techstars portfolio company  
    B. Venture-funded founder  
    C. Attendee at a startup-building event; accelerator admission is a separate claim  
    D. Certified investment-ready founder

### Answer key and routing explanation

| Item | Answer | What it diagnoses |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **C** | Difference between weak attitudinal evidence and behavioral/customer evidence |
| 2 | **B** | Bottom-up market reasoning |
| 3 | **C** | Selection of a decision-relevant real-world test |
| 4 | **B** | Marketplace revenue terminology |
| 5 | **B** | Enterprise buying-system knowledge |
| 6 | **C** | CAC calculation: €60,000 / 30 = €2,000 |
| 7 | **B** | Runway calculation: €600,000 / €75,000 = 8 months |
| 8 | **B** | Post-money value €5m; €1m / €5m = 20%, before other mechanics |
| 9 | **B** | Governance and ownership alignment |
| 10 | **C** | Inventorship versus ownership, license and FTO |
| 11 | **B** | Evidence maturity and calibrated technical claims |
| 12 | **C** | Precise ecosystem-participation claims |

Do not compute a global personality judgment from the total. Route each domain separately.

## 7. Applied scenario test

Use this after the routing screen for founders who demonstrate foundation knowledge.

### Scenario

NovaFlow sells workflow software to mid-sized laboratories. It has:

- €900,000 cash;
- €90,000 current monthly net burn;
- 12 paying customers;
- €24,000 average ARR per customer;
- 75% gross margin;
- €144,000 attributable sales and marketing spend over the last 12 months;
- 8 customers acquired during that period;
- two customers that churned after implementation problems;
- a proposed €1.5 million priced round at €6 million pre-money;
- a planned €500,000 annual increase in operating cost after the financing.

### Questions

1. Calculate current runway assuming burn remains constant.
2. Calculate simple historical CAC under the stated method.
3. Calculate current ARR from the 12 paying customers.
4. What crucial information is missing before interpreting LTV?
5. What is the post-money valuation under the simplified financing assumptions?
6. What approximate ownership percentage would the new investor receive before option-pool or other adjustments?
7. Why might the current runway estimate be misleading?
8. Which evidence should be examined before calling the two churned customers “bad-fit customers”?
9. Propose one experiment to determine whether implementation—not core product value—is causing churn.
10. Name three term-sheet issues that cannot be evaluated from the headline valuation alone.
11. Identify one leading, one lagging and one guardrail metric for the next quarter.
12. State the single most important clarification question you would ask management and explain why.

### Scoring anchors

| Question | Strong-answer elements |
|---|---|
| 1 | 10 months; explicit assumption that burn stays constant |
| 2 | €18,000 CAC; notes chosen cost definition may omit or include disputed costs |
| 3 | €288,000 ARR; distinguishes booked/contracted/collected revenue if relevant |
| 4 | Retention/churn by cohort, gross-profit contribution, expansion/contraction, customer lifetime and servicing cost |
| 5 | €7.5 million simplified post-money |
| 6 | 20% simplified ownership: €1.5m / €7.5m |
| 7 | Planned cost increase, revenue timing, collections, hiring, financing timing and churn can change burn |
| 8 | Onboarding records, usage, support history, promised capabilities, buyer/user fit, implementation ownership and exit interviews |
| 9 | A time-bounded onboarding intervention or controlled comparison with activation and retention criteria |
| 10 | Liquidation preference, option-pool treatment, anti-dilution, board/control, protective provisions, pro rata, vesting or tranche mechanics |
| 11 | Coherent causal chain; e.g. implementation completion → cohort retention/expansion → support burden/security guardrail |
| 12 | Prioritizes a decision-changing unknown and explains its value |

Questions 9, 11 and 12 measure application and prioritization better than vocabulary recall.

## 8. Domain challenge bank

The LLM should draw one or two relevant prompts, not administer all of them.

| Domain | Foundation prompt | Advanced prompt |
|---|---|---|
| Customer | Explain user versus buyer | Rewrite a leading interview question and identify the decision it informs |
| Market | Distinguish TAM, SAM and SOM | Build a bottom-up SOM with assumptions and a sensitivity range |
| Product | Define MVP in terms of learning | Select the cheapest test capable of disproving the highest-risk assumption |
| Business model | Distinguish revenue model and business model | Show how pricing changes behavior, margin, sales motion and support burden |
| GTM | Explain funnel versus pipeline | Map stakeholders and failure points in an enterprise buying process |
| Metrics | Calculate CAC and gross margin | Diagnose when aggregate LTV:CAC hides cohort deterioration |
| Finance | Calculate burn and runway | Build base, downside and financing-delay cases |
| Funding | Explain pre/post-money and dilution | Compare priced equity, SAFE/convertible and grant for a stated venture |
| Team | Explain vesting and decision rights | Resolve a founder deadlock without relying on personality labels |
| Legal/IP | Explain inventor, owner and license | Identify missing rights and counsel questions in a university spin-off |
| Technology | Distinguish prototype, PoP and PoC | Connect technical evidence to regulatory, manufacturing and customer milestones |
| Ecosystem | Distinguish event attendance and accelerator selection | Choose among three programs based on bottleneck, burden and success criterion |

## 9. Scoring model

Report six separate dimensions:

| Dimension | Evidence | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Foundation knowledge** | Correct responses to terminology/concept items | What the founder currently knows in tested domains |
| **Application** | Scenario decisions and calculations | Ability to use knowledge in a realistic case |
| **Transfer** | Applying the principle to a novel or own-venture situation | Flexible understanding rather than memorized jargon |
| **Calibration** | Accuracy aligned with stated confidence | Whether the founder knows when they may be wrong |
| **Learning gain** | Performance on parallel items before and after instruction | What changed during the session |
| **Evidence updating** | Revision after credible contrary evidence | Whether reasoning changes appropriately |

### Suggested provisional reporting bands

These are instructional bands, not validated population norms:

| Band | Description | Learning response |
|---|---|---|
| **Orientation** | Little demonstrated knowledge or insufficient evidence | Teach plain-language foundations with examples |
| **Working vocabulary** | Recognizes concepts but application is inconsistent | Use short scenarios and calculations |
| **Applied** | Correctly applies most concepts in relevant situations | Move to trade-offs, failure cases and venture artifacts |
| **Advanced** | Integrates domains, assumptions and second-order effects | Use adversarial cases, changing constraints and peer teaching |
| **Uncertain** | Too few relevant items or unstable performance | Gather more evidence; do not force a band |

Never compare a life-science research founder's initial SaaS-metrics vocabulary with a SaaS operator's as though it represented general capability.

### Confidence calibration

Store confidence as a probability and calculate calibration only after enough items. A simple Brier score for binary answers is:

$$
\text{Brier} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(p_i-y_i)^2
$$

where $p_i$ is the founder's stated probability of being correct and $y_i$ is 1 for correct and 0 for incorrect. Lower is better. Show the founder a plain-language explanation, not only a number.

## 10. How to observe curiosity responsibly

### What the knowledge test cannot establish

A high jargon score may reflect prior employment, education, investment access, English fluency or repeated accelerator exposure. A low score may reflect a new domain, different jurisdiction or lack of opportunity. Neither establishes curiosity.

### Better behavioral indicators during a voluntary learning task

| Indicator | Observable behavior | Safeguard |
|---|---|---|
| Question generation | Produces relevant questions that reduce uncertainty | Do not reward talkativeness or culturally specific conversational style |
| Information-gap recognition | Identifies what cannot be concluded from available evidence | Allow written or asynchronous responses |
| Challenge selection | Voluntarily attempts an appropriately harder item | Do not penalize rational time management or opting out |
| Evidence seeking | Requests the source or proposes a way to test a claim | Judge relevance and method, not number of searches |
| Updating | Changes a conclusion after credible contradictory evidence | Do not reward agreement with the interviewer when evidence is weak |
| Transfer exploration | Connects a learned concept to a new setting | Separate valid transfer from confident analogy |
| Follow-through | Returns to an open question with better evidence | Require a reasonable opportunity and time window |

Use a descriptive curiosity panel initially. Do not produce a single curiosity score until the construct, observations, raters and fairness have been validated for the exact context.

Recent curiosity research supports a relationship between curiosity and learning but does not validate inferring founder curiosity from startup terminology. The system should therefore treat curiosity as a hypothesis supported by repeated behavior, never as a diagnosis.

## 11. How to observe capability responsibly

Capability is domain- and stage-specific. Prefer work samples:

- design a customer-discovery guide;
- calculate runway and a downside case;
- critique a market estimate;
- interpret retention cohorts;
- compare two funding offers;
- identify missing IP rights;
- write a 30-day pilot with success and stop criteria;
- map founder decision rights;
- prioritize three risks under a fixed budget;
- explain the same technology to a customer and a technical expert.

Rate the artifact using explicit criteria: correctness, relevance, assumptions, evidence, trade-offs, uncertainty and actionability. Jargon is optional if the reasoning is clear.

## 12. LLM interview behavior

### Tone and teaching

- Ask permission before beginning a test.
- Explain that it personalizes learning and is not an intelligence/personality test.
- Permit “I don't know,” skipping and requesting a definition.
- Avoid correcting every minor wording difference when the concept is right.
- Use the founder's jurisdiction and venture type.
- Praise effective reasoning or updating, not identity or innate talent.
- Give short explanations first; offer depth only when requested or needed.

### Adaptive interview pseudocode

```text
collect_preferences_and_consent()
for domain in relevant_domains:
    response, confidence = ask(seed_item(domain))
    if correct(response) and confidence >= .90:
        ask(application_item(domain, harder=True))
    elif incorrect(response) and confidence >= .90:
        teach(misconception_explanation(domain))
        ask(parallel_application_item(domain))
    elif incorrect(response):
        teach(foundation_explanation(domain))
        ask(foundation_retrieval_item(domain))
    else:
        ask(confirming_item(domain))
    stop_when_mastered_unstable_irrelevant_or_time_limited()
report_separate_dimensions_with_uncertainty()
recommend_at_most_three_learning_actions()
```

### Prohibited LLM shortcuts

- Do not generate a new question and call it validated.
- Do not infer knowledge from vocabulary density in normal conversation.
- Do not assume fluent English means higher startup capability.
- Do not lower the test because a founder communicates concisely.
- Do not teach to the answer and reuse the same item as a post-test.
- Do not expose private wrong answers to investors by default.
- Do not infer personality from hesitation, response speed, grammar or accent.
- Do not use social-media startup language as evidence that a founder understands it.

## 13. Scientific basis and limits

### Supported design choices

- The 2024 [meta-analysis of computerized adaptive testing](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1027/1015-5759/a000821) supports examining CAT efficiency and measurement effects, but does not validate this particular founder item bank.
- A 2024 [meta-analysis of formative assessment](https://doi.org/10.1080/13803611.2024.2363831) synthesized 118 primary studies and reported a positive overall learning effect. Its K–12 setting limits direct generalization to adult founders, but it supports using feedback for learning rather than only ranking.
- A 2024 [systematic review of educational technology in entrepreneurship education](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1433111.pdf) found promising results alongside limited evidence, reinforcing the need for careful validation in entrepreneurial populations.
- The [EntreComp framework](https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu/repository/handle/JRC101581) provides broad content coverage across ideas/opportunities, resources and action. It should guide the curriculum, not be represented as a validated adaptive test.

### What science does not currently justify

- a universal founder-jargon score predicting startup success;
- treating terminology as a proxy for intelligence, curiosity, resilience or ethics;
- comparing scores across countries without language and measurement-invariance testing;
- using an unvalidated LLM interview for investment, employment or accelerator exclusion;
- claiming that short-term quiz improvement predicts venture execution.

## 14. Validation plan before consequential use

### Stage 1 — content and usability

1. Define intended decisions and explicitly exclude personality diagnosis.
2. Have founders, operators, investors, educators, lawyers and finance specialists review domain coverage.
3. Conduct cognitive interviews with novice and experienced founders in Germany and the United States.
4. Review translations and jurisdiction-specific terms.
5. Pilot accessibility modes and non-native-language use.

### Stage 2 — item quality

1. Use parallel forms for pre/post learning checks.
2. Estimate item difficulty and discrimination.
3. Remove trick questions and items driven by obscure wording.
4. Examine distractor functioning and confidently held misconceptions.
5. Calibrate an item-response model only after an adequate, representative sample exists.
6. Prevent overexposure and memorization of high-value items.

### Stage 3 — reliability and validity

1. Test domain score reliability and test–retest stability where stability is expected.
2. Test content, response-process and internal-structure evidence.
3. Compare with independent work-sample ratings.
4. Test incremental validity beyond prior role, education, sector and opportunity.
5. Analyze measurement invariance across language, country, gender, age and relevant access groups.
6. Test whether adaptive paths produce comparable scores and user experiences.
7. Document false-positive and false-negative consequences.

### Stage 4 — governance

1. Publish a construct map, item-development policy and model card.
2. Version every item and source.
3. Keep learning mode separate from selection mode.
4. Require human review for consequential interpretation.
5. Give founders access, correction, deletion and an appeal route.
6. Revalidate after material changes to the item bank, adaptive algorithm or target population.

Until these steps are completed, label results **developmental and non-validated**.

## 15. Fairness, privacy and accessibility

- Offer German and English, with conceptual rather than literal translation.
- Permit audio, text, extended time, screen-reader and low-bandwidth formats.
- Do not use response speed unless speed is explicitly relevant and validated.
- Ask only venture-relevant knowledge; skip financing jargon for founders not pursuing external financing unless they want to learn it.
- Separate U.S., German and cross-border legal terminology.
- Collect the minimum personal data and state who can see the results.
- Do not use protected characteristics or proxies to change difficulty or score.
- Do not penalize a founder for declining to share learning telemetry.
- Provide the explanation and learning resources even when results are not shared.
- Audit differential item functioning and completion/dropout patterns.
- Never market the assessment as psychological, clinical or success-predictive without corresponding evidence.

## 17. Recommended first implementation

Build the MVP in this order:

1. founder-controlled setup and consent;
2. the 12-item routing screen;
3. the NovaFlow applied scenario;
4. short explanations linked to each misconception;
5. three-domain personalized learning path;
6. founder-only report;
7. expert content review and cognitive interviews;
8. pilot data collection;
9. only then, psychometric adaptation and any optional sharing with evaluators.

Do not begin with an opaque LLM-generated score. Begin with transparent questions, explicit answers, useful explanations and founder control.

## Bottom line

An adaptive founder-world test can make the interview more useful and less boring. Experienced founders should quickly reach realistic cases; beginners should receive a welcoming foundation and a chance to demonstrate how they learn.

The most defensible evaluation is not “How many startup acronyms did this person already know?” It is: **What did they understand, how did they apply it, did they recognize uncertainty, and how effectively did they learn when given relevant evidence?**
